Technical Guide — Choosing the Right Clinch Stud

Selecting the correct self clinching stud type depends on three factors: the sheet material, the required sheet hardness, and the environment where the finished assembly will operate. FH-type studs (heat-treated carbon steel, zinc plated) cover the vast majority of applications — electronics, switchgear, telecom and general sheet metal fabrication in India. FHS-type studs (300-series stainless steel, passivated) are specified when the stud itself must resist corrosion in outdoor, marine or wet environments. Both types install flush — the head disappears into the panel surface, leaving only a clean protruding threaded shank.

FH

FH-Type — Carbon Steel

The standard workhorse stud for indoor sheet metal fabrication. Heat-treated carbon steel core with zinc plating provides excellent hardness for reliable clinching in steel and aluminium panels.

  • Material: Heat-treated carbon steel
  • Finish: Zinc plated (ASTM B633)
  • Sheet limit: HRB 80 / HB 150
  • Best for: Electronics, telecom, switchboards
FHS

FHS-Type — Stainless Steel

300-series stainless steel body — fully corrosion resistant. Ideal when the stud itself is exposed to moisture, chemicals or coastal salt air. Softer than FH-type, so requires a lower-hardness host panel.

  • Material: 300-series stainless steel
  • Finish: Passivated
  • Sheet limit: HRB 70 / HB 125
  • Best for: Outdoor, coastal, food-grade
FH4

Stainless Panel Applications

FH and FHS type studs cannot be installed in stainless steel panels — stainless sheets are typically HRB 90+, harder than the stud. A 400-series hardened stainless stud (PEM FH4 equivalent) is required for stainless host panels.

  • Material: 400-series hardened stainless
  • Finish: As supplied
  • Sheet limit: HRB 90 / HB 192

Clinch Stud vs Clinch Nut — Which Do You Need?

This is the most common selection question. The answer depends entirely on whether you need a male thread or a female thread on your panel.

Choose Clinch STUD when…

  • ✔ You want a male thread projecting from the panel
  • ✔ A nut will be threaded onto the stud to attach components
  • ✔ You cannot access the back of the panel to hold a nut
  • ✔ You need multiple assemblies to stack onto fixed panel bosses
  • ✔ Example: PCB standoff studs, bracket mounting bosses, panel hinges

Choose Clinch NUT when…

  • ✔ You want a female thread (socket) in the panel
  • ✔ A bolt or screw will thread into the panel from the outside
  • ✔ The panel is too thin to tap threads directly
  • ✔ You need a retained, permanent thread that won't strip
  • ✔ Example: enclosure lid screws, access panel fastening points

How the Self Clinching Stud Works

The self clinching stud works through controlled cold-forming of the host sheet. The stud has three functional zones: the ribbed head, which embeds into the sheet surface; the annular groove, into which displaced sheet metal flows to lock the stud axially; and the threaded shank, which protrudes above the opposite panel face for nut attachment.

When a squeezing force is applied by a press, the head ribs bite into the sheet surface and simultaneously displace sheet metal radially inward into the annular groove. This creates a mechanical interlock that resists pull-out and rotation. The stud head ends up embedded completely flush on one side while the threaded shank protrudes cleanly on the other. The critical requirement is differential hardness — the stud must always be significantly harder than the host sheet for the ribs to displace the material correctly. If the sheet is too hard, the ribs cannot bite and push-out resistance will be negligible.

Installation — Critical Rules

Self clinching studs must be installed by a squeezing force — a bench press, C-frame press, or hydraulic/pneumatic press with a flat punch and anvil. The press must apply force perpendicular to the sheet. Never use a hammer or impact tool — uneven force will cock the stud or crack the sheet. The anvil must support the sheet completely around the hole to prevent distortion.

Install into bare, uncoated sheet — always

Never install self clinching studs into painted or powder-coated panels. Coating fills the mounting hole and prevents the sheet metal from flowing into the annular groove — push-out resistance will be near zero. Always install into bare sheet metal first, then carry out any painting, powder coating or anodising on the complete assembly. Do not deburr or chamfer the mounting hole — deburring removes the material that must flow around the stud ribs. Always install from the punch side (the side that will be flush) for best results.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a self clinching stud and how does it work?

A self clinching stud provides a permanent external (male) thread projecting from a thin sheet metal panel. The ribbed head is pressed into a punched hole with a squeezing force. Sheet metal displaced by the head flows into the annular groove — permanently locking the stud with high push-out and torque-out resistance. A nut is then threaded onto the protruding shank to attach components. No welding, heat or adhesive required — the head sits flush after installation and the stud thread is fully reusable.

What is the difference between PEM FH-type and FHS-type clinch studs?

FH-type studs are heat-treated carbon steel with zinc plating — the standard choice for electronics, switchboards and general sheet metal fabrication in India. They install in steel and aluminium panels up to HRB 80 / HB 150. FHS-type studs are 300-series stainless steel, passivated — fully corrosion resistant but softer, working only in panels up to HRB 70 / HB 125. Choose FH-type for indoor dry applications. Choose FHS-type when the stud must resist corrosion in outdoor, wet or coastal environments.

What is the minimum sheet thickness for self clinching studs?

PEM FH and FHS type flush-head clinch studs install flush in sheets from 1.0mm thickness. The head depth (H dimension) varies by size — M3 has H of approximately 4.6mm, M4 approximately 5.9mm, M5 approximately 6.5mm, M6 approximately 8.2mm. The sheet must be at least as thick as the head depth for the stud to sit flush. Using a sheet thinner than the minimum will leave the head partially above the panel surface and seriously reduce push-out resistance.

Why must clinch studs be installed before painting or powder coating?

Paint and powder coating fill the mounting hole and occupy the space where sheet metal must flow around the stud's locking ribs and into the annular groove. If coating is present, the metal cannot displace properly — push-out and torque-out resistance will be severely reduced. Always install clinch studs into bare, uncoated sheet metal first, then coat the complete assembly. Similarly, do not deburr the mounting hole — deburring removes the material needed for cold-forming around the ribs.

Can I use a clinch stud in a stainless steel panel?

Not with FH-type or FHS-type studs. Stainless steel panels are typically HRB 90 or harder — which exceeds the installation limit for both types. The stud must be harder than the host sheet for the ribs to displace material. In stainless sheets, neither FH nor FHS studs will grip adequately and push-out resistance will be near zero. A 400-series hardened stainless stud (PEM FH4 equivalent) is required for stainless host panels. Contact Excel Trading Corporation to discuss the correct specification for your application.
