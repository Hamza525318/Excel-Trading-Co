    Self Clinching Nut — Press Nut

    PEM S-Type & CLS-Type Equivalent  ·  M3 to M8  ·  Sheet Metal from 0.5mm  ·  Zinc Plated & Stainless Steel

    The self clinching nut — also called a press nut or clinch nut — is the standard solution for creating permanent, load-bearing internal (female) threads in thin sheet metal panels. When pressed into a precisely punched hole using a squeezing force, the nut's serrated clinching ring causes the surrounding sheet metal to cold-flow into its annular undercut groove, permanently locking the fastener in place. The result is a reusable, high-strength threaded insert that is permanently part of the panel — flush on the head side, no protrusion, no loose hardware.

    Excel Trading Corporation supplies PEM S-type equivalent (hardened carbon steel, zinc plated) and PEM CLS-type equivalent (300-series stainless steel) clinch nuts in M3, M4, M5, M6 and M8 — the two most widely used self clinching nut types in sheet metal fabrication across India. No welding, no heat, no adhesive — only a simple press is required.

    Applications

    • Electronics enclosures — server chassis, networking hardware, control panels requiring permanent threads in 1–2mm sheet
    • Electrical switchboard fabrication — DIN rail mounting, busbar supports and cover plates in sheet steel
    • Telecom rack enclosures and outdoor cabinets — flush installation keeps panel interiors clean
    • Precision sheet metal panels too thin to tap conventionally — typical in aluminium and mild steel enclosures
    • Automotive brackets and subframe panels where welded nuts are not practical
    • Industrial machine guards, junction boxes and relay panels

    Key Features

    • Permanent internal thread — flush head after installation, clean reverse side
    • Serrated clinching ring provides high torque-out resistance — nut cannot rotate under load
    • Cold-formed undercut locking delivers high push-out resistance from the panel
    • No welding, heat or adhesive — installed with bench press, hydraulic or pneumatic press
    • Reusable thread — survives repeated assembly and disassembly without thread degradation
    • Two material options: S-type zinc plated carbon steel and CLS-type 300-series stainless steel

    Self Clinching Nut — Complete Technical Guide

    How the Self Clinching Nut Works

    The self clinching nut has three functional zones working together to create a permanent, high-strength installation:

    The pilot (shank) is the lower cylindrical section that fits into the punched hole. Its diameter is closely controlled — the hole must be punched to a specific size for the nut to function correctly. The clinching ring sits above the pilot and features fine serrations or knurling around its circumference. When a squeezing force is applied from a press, this ring is driven into the top surface of the sheet. As force continues, the sheet metal at the hole edge cold-flows radially inward and downward into the annular undercut groove between the pilot and the clinching ring body. This displaced metal locks the nut permanently against pull-out. The serrations on the clinching ring bite into the sheet surface, preventing any rotation under torque. The head comes into contact with the sheet surface and stops the installation — the nut is now flush on the head side and the undercut has been fully filled with displaced metal on the shank side.

    The fundamental requirement is differential hardness: the nut must always be harder than the host sheet. If the sheet is too hard, the clinching ring will not adequately displace the material, resulting in very low push-out resistance. This is why S-type (max HRB 80) and CLS-type (max HRB 70) have different hardness limits — CLS stainless steel is softer than S hardened carbon steel, so it can only be used in softer panels.

    Understanding Shank Codes — Choosing the Right Length

    Every self clinching nut comes in multiple shank codes (-1, -2, sometimes -3). The shank code determines the length of the shank and therefore the minimum sheet thickness the nut can be used with. A longer shank means more metal is available to be displaced into the undercut — giving higher push-out resistance, but requiring a thicker sheet.

    -1
    Shank Code
    Min sheet: 0.5mm
    Shortest shank. Use for very thin panels — 0.5mm to ~1.0mm. Lower push-out values. Best for aluminium enclosure sheet.
    -2
    Shank Code — Most Common
    Min sheet: 1.0mm
    Standard shank. Best for 1.0mm–2.0mm sheet — the most common thickness range in electronics and switchboard fabrication. Higher push-out resistance than -1.
    -3
    Shank Code
    Min sheet: 1.4mm
    Long shank. Available in M8 and above. Maximum push-out and torque-out values. For heavy-duty structural panels 1.4mm+.

    Pro tip — always use maximum shank for your thickness

    PEM's own recommendation is to use the longest shank code that your sheet thickness allows. If your sheet is 1.2mm, use shank code -2 (min 1.0mm) rather than -1. The longer shank displaces more material into the undercut, giving significantly higher push-out and torque-out values.

    Step-by-Step Installation Guide

    01

    Punch the Hole

    Punch a precisely sized round hole in the sheet. Use the hole size specified for your nut's thread size and shank code. Do not deburr or chamfer — deburring removes the material that must cold-flow into the undercut.

    02

    Position in Press

    Place the nut in the counterbored anvil hole with the shank facing up. Position the sheet's mounting hole over the shank, preferably from the punch side of the sheet. Ensure the sheet is held level and parallel to both punch and anvil surfaces.

    03

    Apply Squeezing Force

    Apply a parallel squeezing force until the nut head comes into full contact with the sheet and the clinching ring is fully embedded. Do not over-squeeze — this will crush the head, distort the thread and buckle the panel.

    04

    Verify Installation

    The nut head must be flush with the panel surface — no rocking, no gap. Test by threading in a bolt and attempting to rotate the nut by hand. If it rotates, insufficient force was applied or the sheet hardness exceeded the nut's limit.

    ✓ Do's

    • ✓Install into bare, uncoated sheet metal — before painting or powder coating
    • ✓Use the exact punched hole size specified for your nut size and shank code
    • ✓Keep punch and anvil surfaces perfectly parallel during installation
    • ✓Use the maximum shank code that your sheet thickness allows
    • ✓Install before any secondary forming, bending or finishing operations
    • ✓Verify sheet hardness does not exceed the nut type's maximum (HRB 80 for S, HRB 70 for CLS)

    ✗ Don'ts

    • ✗Never install on a pre-painted or powder-coated panel — coating blocks the undercut
    • ✗Never deburr or chamfer the mounting hole — this removes the metal needed for locking
    • ✗Never over-squeeze — crushed head, distorted thread and buckled panel will result
    • ✗Never install S or CLS type nuts into stainless steel sheets — use SP-type only
    • ✗Never install steel or stainless nuts in aluminium before anodising — install first, then anodise
    • ✗Never position a nut closer to the sheet edge than the minimum edge distance in the datasheet

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is a self clinching nut and how does it work?

    A self clinching nut provides a permanent internal (female) thread in a thin sheet metal panel. It is pressed into a punched hole by a squeezing force. The sheet metal cold-flows into the nut's annular undercut groove, locking it permanently with high push-out and torque-out resistance. A serrated clinching ring prevents rotation. The head side is flush after installation. No welding or heat required.

    What is the difference between PEM S-type and CLS-type self clinching nuts?

    S-type nuts are made from hardened carbon steel with zinc plating. They work in steel and aluminium panels up to HRB 80 / HB 150 — the standard choice for electronics, switchboards and general fabrication. CLS-type nuts are made from 300-series stainless steel. They are corrosion resistant but work only in softer panels up to HRB 70 / HB 125. Use CLS-type when the fastener must resist corrosion in outdoor, wet or coastal environments.

    What is the minimum sheet thickness required for a self clinching nut?

    Minimum sheet thickness depends on the shank code. Shank code -1 works from 0.5mm (M3–M5). Shank code -2 requires minimum 1.0mm (M3–M8). Shank code -3 requires minimum 1.4mm (M8). Always use the longest shank code your sheet thickness allows — it gives the highest push-out resistance. The sheet must be bare uncoated metal and within the hardness limit for your chosen type.

    Why should self clinching nuts not be installed in painted panels?

    Paint or powder coating fills the mounting hole and occupies the space where sheet metal must cold-flow into the undercut. If the coating is present, the metal cannot displace properly, and push-out and torque-out resistance will be drastically reduced or near zero. Always install into bare, uncoated sheet. If the panel will be coated, install the nuts first, then carry out the coating process on the full assembly.

    Can self clinching nuts be installed into stainless steel panels?

    Not with S-type or CLS-type nuts. Stainless steel panels are harder than standard steel — typically HRB 90+ — which exceeds the maximum sheet hardness for both S and CLS types. For stainless steel panels, a specially hardened SP-type nut (PEM SP equivalent) is required. Contact us to discuss if you need clinching hardware for stainless steel panel applications.
