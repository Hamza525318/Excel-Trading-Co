    Self Clinching Nut — Press Nut

    PEM S-Type & CLS-Type Equivalent  ·  M3 to M8  ·  Sheet Metal from 0.5mm  ·  Zinc Plated & Stainless Steel

    The self clinching nut — also called a press nut or clinch nut — is the standard solution for creating permanent, reusable internal (female) threads in thin sheet metal panels. When pressed into a precisely punched hole, the nut's serrated clinching ring causes the surrounding sheet metal to cold-flow into its annular undercut groove, locking it permanently with high push-out and torque-out resistance. The head is flush after installation — no protrusion, no loose hardware, no welding required. Excel Trading Corporation stocks the two most widely used types globally: the PEM S-type equivalent in hardened carbon steel (zinc plated) and the PEM CLS-type equivalent in 300-series stainless steel, in M3 through M8 thread sizes.

    Applications

    • Electronics enclosures — server chassis, networking hardware, control panels with 1–2mm sheet
    • Electrical switchboard fabrication — DIN rail mounting, busbar supports, cover plates
    • Telecom rack enclosures and outdoor cabinets — flush installation keeps interiors clean
    • Precision sheet metal panels too thin to tap conventionally — aluminium and mild steel
    • Automotive brackets and subframe panels where welded nuts are not practical
    • Industrial machine guards, junction boxes and relay panels

    Features

    • Permanent internal thread — flush head after installation, clean reverse side
    • Serrated clinching ring provides high torque-out resistance — cannot rotate under load
    • Cold-formed undercut locking delivers high push-out resistance from the panel
    • No welding, heat or adhesive — installed with bench press, hydraulic or pneumatic press
    • Reusable thread — survives repeated assembly and disassembly without degradation
    • Two types: S-type hardened carbon steel (zinc plated) and CLS-type 300-series stainless steel

    Self Clinching Nut — Technical Guide

    How the Self Clinching Nut Works

    The self clinching nut works through controlled cold-forming of the host sheet. It has three functional zones: the pilot (shank), which fits into the punched hole; the serrated clinching ring, which bites into the sheet surface and prevents rotation; and the annular undercut groove between the pilot and the ring body. When a squeezing force is applied, the clinching ring is driven into the sheet surface while simultaneously forcing the sheet metal at the hole edge to flow radially inward and downward into the undercut. This displaced metal permanently locks the nut against pull-out. The nut head contacts the sheet and stops the press stroke — the nut is now flush on the head side and permanently locked on the shank side.

    The critical requirement is differential hardness — the nut must always be significantly harder than the host sheet. If the sheet is too hard, the clinching ring cannot displace enough metal into the undercut and push-out resistance will be near zero. This is why S-type and CLS-type have different sheet hardness limits — S hardened carbon steel is harder than CLS stainless, so it works in harder panels.

    Type S — Carbon Steel

    Zinc Plated · Hardened · Standard Grade

    Made from hardened carbon steel with zinc plating. The standard clinch nut for the vast majority of applications — electronics, switchboards, telecom and general precision fabrication. Works in steel and aluminium panels up to HRB 80 / HB 150. Significantly harder than CLS-type stainless, giving maximum displacement force and the highest push-out values for a given sheet thickness. Most cost-effective option.

    Best for: Server chassis, switchboards, relay panels, telecom enclosures, automotive brackets — all indoor and dry environments.

    Type CLS — Stainless Steel

    300-Series SS · Passivated · Corrosion Resistant

    Made from 300-series austenitic stainless steel, passivated. Fully corrosion resistant in wet, coastal and chemical environments. Because 300-series stainless is softer than hardened carbon steel, CLS-type works only in softer panels — maximum sheet hardness HRB 70 / HB 125. Standard cold-rolled steel typically falls within this limit. Do not use in harder steel grades.

    Best for: Outdoor telecom cabinets, coastal installations, food processing panels, marine enclosures — anywhere the fastener must resist corrosion long-term.

    Stainless Steel Panels

    Neither S nor CLS — use SP-type

    Stainless steel panels are typically HRB 90 or harder — exceeding the maximum sheet hardness for both S-type and CLS-type nuts. Attempting to install either type into stainless sheet will result in near-zero push-out resistance. For stainless steel panels, a specially hardened SP-type nut (PEM SP equivalent) is required.     Shank Codes — Selecting the Right Length for Your Panel Thickness

    Every self clinching nut is available in multiple shank codes (-1, -2, -3). The shank code defines the pilot length and the minimum sheet thickness required. A longer shank means more sheet metal cold-flows into the undercut — giving higher push-out resistance. Always use the longest shank code that your sheet thickness allows.

    Critical installation rule — always install into bare, uncoated sheet

    Never install self clinching nuts into painted or powder-coated panels. Coating fills the punched hole and prevents the sheet metal from cold-flowing into the undercut — push-out resistance will be near zero. Install into bare sheet metal first, then carry out any painting or powder coating on the full assembly. Also — never deburr or chamfer the mounting hole. Deburring removes the material that must flow into the undercut.

    Frequently Asked Questions

    What is a self clinching nut and how does it work?

    A self clinching nut provides a permanent internal (female) thread in a thin sheet metal panel. It is pressed into a punched hole by a squeezing force from a press. The sheet metal cold-flows into the nut's annular undercut groove, locking it permanently with high push-out and torque-out resistance. A serrated clinching ring prevents rotation under load. The head side is flush after installation. No welding, heat or adhesive required — and the thread is fully reusable.

    What is the difference between PEM S-type and CLS-type self clinching nuts?

    S-type nuts are hardened carbon steel with zinc plating — the standard choice for electronics, switchboards and general fabrication. They work in steel and aluminium panels up to HRB 80 / HB 150. CLS-type nuts are 300-series stainless steel — fully corrosion resistant but softer, so they work only in panels up to HRB 70 / HB 125. Choose S-type for indoor dry applications. Choose CLS-type when the fastener must resist corrosion in outdoor, wet or coastal environments.

    What is the minimum sheet thickness required for a self clinching nut?

    Minimum sheet thickness depends on the shank code. Shank code -1 works from 0.5mm for M3 to M5. Shank code -2 requires a minimum of 1.0mm for M3 to M6. M8 shank code -2 requires 1.0mm and shank code -3 requires 1.4mm. Always use the longest shank code your sheet allows — it gives significantly higher push-out resistance. The sheet must also be bare, uncoated metal within the hardness limit for your chosen nut type.

    Why must self clinching nuts be installed before painting or powder coating?

    Paint and powder coating fill the mounting hole and occupy the space where sheet metal must cold-flow into the nut's undercut. If coating is present, the metal cannot displace properly — push-out and torque-out resistance will be severely reduced or near zero. Always install self clinching nuts into bare, uncoated sheet. If the panel will be finished after fabrication, install the nuts first, then coat the complete assembly.

    Can self clinching nuts be installed into stainless steel panels?

    Not with S-type or CLS-type nuts. Stainless steel panels are typically HRB 90 or harder — which exceeds the maximum sheet hardness for both types. If installed, the clinching ring will not displace the panel material adequately and push-out resistance will be near zero. For stainless steel panels, a specially hardened SP-type nut (PEM SP equivalent) is required. Contact us to discuss the correct specification for stainless sheet applications.
