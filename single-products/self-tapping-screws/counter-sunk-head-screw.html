The CSK (countersunk) head self tapping screw confirming to DIN 7982 C / ISO 7050 is a fully threaded fastener with a cross recessed Phillips drive and a gimlet point that cuts its own thread into pre-drilled sheet metal, plastic, fibre glass and wood — no nut, no pre-tapped hole required. The flat, conical head seats flush with the material surface when driven into a countersunk hole, giving a smooth, snag-free finish. Installation Tips

  • ▸ Always drill a pilot hole — never drive a self tapping screw into undrilled material. An undersized pilot creates excessive torque and risks cam-out or head stripping.
  • ▸ Countersink the pilot hole to match the head angle so the CSK head seats flush — skipping this step leaves the head proud or cracks brittle materials around the hole.
  • ▸ Use the correct pilot hole size from the table above — too large reduces thread engagement; too small risks splitting plastic or snapping the screw.
  • ▸ Do not overtighten — driving a CSK screw too far below flush weakens its grip and can strip the drive recess. Stop as soon as the head sits level with the surface.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a CSK (countersunk) head self tapping screw?

A CSK head self tapping screw has a flat, conical head that sits flush with or slightly below the material surface once driven into a countersunk hole. The self tapping thread cuts its own thread into a pre-drilled hole in sheet metal, plastic, fibre glass or wood — no nut or tapping tool required. Use it wherever a projecting head would snag, look untidy or interfere with a mating surface. Standardised under DIN 7982 and ISO 7050 with Phillips cross drive.

What is the difference between DIN 7982 C and ISO 7050?

DIN 7982 Form C is the German standard and ISO 7050 is the international equivalent for the same countersunk cross recessed self tapping screw with a sharp thread-cutting point. Dimensions, thread form (ISO 1478) and head geometry are identical — they are interchangeable. Both designations are widely used and accepted in India. Excel Trading Corporation stocks screws compliant with both standards.

Do I need to countersink the hole, and what pilot hole size do I use?

Yes — countersink (chamfer) the pilot hole to match the head angle so the screw sits flush; skipping this leaves the head proud or can crack brittle materials. For the pilot hole diameter itself, refer to the Pilot Hole column in the size table above — the same core-diameter pilot sizes apply as for other head styles since sizing depends on thread diameter, not head shape. Use a slightly smaller pilot in plastic or wood for better thread engagement.

When should I use stainless steel instead of mild steel?

Use stainless steel (A2/SS304) whenever the screw will be exposed to moisture, outdoor conditions, coastal air or food contact. Zinc plated mild steel corrodes over time in these environments — the zinc layer is not sufficient for prolonged wet exposure. For aggressive chemical environments, request SS316 (A4). For all indoor, dry and sheltered applications, zinc plated mild steel is the correct and more economical choice.
