Installation Tips

  • ▸ Always drill a pilot hole — never drive a self tapping screw into undrilled material. An undersized pilot creates excessive torque and risks cam-out or head stripping.
  • ▸ Use the correct pilot hole size from the table above — too large reduces thread engagement; too small risks splitting plastic or snapping the screw.
  • ▸ Drive at moderate speed — high RPM causes heat build-up in plastic that strips the thread before full seating.
  • ▸ Do not overtighten — the pan head's flat underside seats cleanly when hand-tight. Over-driving distorts thin sheet metal and reduces holding strength.

Frequently Asked Questions

What is a pan head self tapping screw?

A pan head self tapping screw has a broad cylindrical head with a flat underside that seats flush against the material surface, distributing clamping load without cutting into the workpiece. The self tapping thread cuts its own thread into a pre-drilled hole in sheet metal, plastic, fibre glass or wood — no nut or tapping tool required. Standardised under DIN 7981 and ISO 7049 with Phillips cross drive.

What is the difference between DIN 7981 and ISO 7049?

DIN 7981 is the German standard and ISO 7049 is the international equivalent for the same pan head cross recessed self tapping screw. The dimensions, thread form (ISO 1478) and mechanical requirements are identical — they are interchangeable. Both designations are widely used and accepted in India. Excel Trading Corporation stocks screws compliant with both standards.

What pilot hole size do I use?

Refer to the Pilot Hole column in the size table above. As a general rule, the pilot hole should be slightly smaller than the screw's outer thread diameter — typically equal to the core diameter. For softer materials like plastic and wood, use a tighter pilot for more thread engagement. For harder sheet metal, the listed pilot holes give clean threading without excessive torque.

When should I use stainless steel instead of mild steel?

Use stainless steel (A2/SS304) whenever the screw will be exposed to moisture, outdoor conditions, coastal air or food contact. Zinc plated mild steel corrodes over time in these environments — the zinc layer is not sufficient for prolonged wet exposure. For aggressive chemical environments, request SS316 (A4). For all indoor, dry and sheltered applications, zinc plated mild steel is the correct and more economical choice.
